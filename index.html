Capstone Project

Crafting the Critical Seal

Within Hexagon Agility's focus on clean fuel solutions for commercial vehicles, our capstone team addressed a key challenge in their fuel systems: the complex and time-consuming assembly of the piping connecting the gas cylinders. Our project, the "CNG Modular Plumbing" aimed to streamline this crucial connection. My primary focus quickly became the novel seal interface – a seemingly small component within this larger system, yet one with significant implications for achieving faster assembly, maintaining safety, and boosting overall production efficiency.

Why the Seal? (Objectives)

Non-Engineering Perspective

The seal is the unsung hero preventing dangerous gas leaks. It needs to be robust and reliable, ensuring the system operates safely for everyone.

Engineering Perspective

The objective was to design a seal that could withstand specific pressure requirements, maintain its integrity over time, and be manufacturable within project constraints.

Early discussions highlighted the critical nature of a leak-proof connection, and I recognized the seal as a pivotal element in achieving this core objective.

How We Got There (Process and Contributions)

My journey with the seal was highly iterative, moving from initial concepts to a refined prototype.

Initial Brainstorming & Low-Fidelity Concepts

Initial sketches exploring flat gasket, O-ring, and wrap-around seal concepts

My initial thoughts revolved around standard sealing solutions like flat gaskets and O-rings. However, early feedback and a deeper understanding of the unique interface geometry suggested these might not provide the optimal long-term seal. I began exploring more unconventional approaches, including a wrap-around design to better conform to the mating surfaces.

First Digital Model & 3D Printing

First iteration CAD model incorporating a simple seal design for initial fit testing

First 3D printed prototype used to assess basic fit and identify potential interference issues

I translated one of the more promising concepts into a basic 3D model. The objective here was primarily for fit testing. I utilized our university's Maker Space to produce a physical prototype using FDM 3D printing. This initial print highlighted several areas for improvement, particularly regarding the seal's contact area and potential for movement under pressure.

Incorporating Client Feedback & Design Iteration

3D print showing the evolution of the seal design, incorporating feedback on contact area and potential for movement

Crucially, feedback from our client, Hexagon Agility, proved invaluable. They suggested an alternative geometry with a through-hole, and the potential use of round stock material. This shifted my focus significantly. The objective became creating a seal that utilized these specific features for enhanced sealing performance.

Revised CAD model implementing the client's suggestions

Material Considerations & Supplier Engagement

Seal fit testing & correspondence with Gasket Tech helped explore suitable elastomeric materials for the final seal prototype

Recognizing the limitations of 3D-printed plastic for pressure testing, the focus shifted to identifying appropriate seal materials. I researched various elastomers and their properties, considering factors like pressure rating, chemical compatibility with natural gas, and availability. I engaged with Gasket Tech to explore potential suppliers and material options for the final seal prototype.

Preparing for Metal Prototype & Pressure Testing

Animated overview of the seal design's evolution, demonstrating the iterative process driven by feedback and testing

Although our initial scope involved 3D-printed prototypes for design validation, we successfully progressed to a fully functional metal prototype incorporating our final seal design. This critical step allowed us to conduct rigorous pressure testing at the Hexagon Agility site, where our seal performed flawlessly up to 1,000 PSI. The insights gained from our earlier 3D-printed models were instrumental in refining the design and ensuring the ultimate success of the seal in the final metal prototype and during high-pressure testing.

Learning and Growth

This iterative process taught me the importance of:

  • Early Prototyping: Quickly creating physical models, even rudimentary ones, to identify fit issues and gather tangible feedback.
  • Client Collaboration: Actively incorporating client expertise and requirements into the design process.
  • Adaptability: Being willing to pivot and explore new design directions based on testing and feedback.
  • Material Awareness: Understanding the critical role material selection plays in the functionality and safety of an engineering component.

My work on the seal, while focused on a single component, was integral to the overall success of the CNG Modular Plumbing project. It demonstrates how a seemingly simple element can undergo significant evolution through a process of ideation, prototyping, testing, and continuous improvement.
